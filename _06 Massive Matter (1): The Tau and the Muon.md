

Let us now discuss in detail how matter actually forms.

Matter is twist-force locked into a topological structure.

But this raises a paradox: a spiral pulse, just like light, runs along a string at the maximum speed (the speed of light).

If it always moves forward at the speed of light, how can the trailing part ever catch up to the leading part and close into a loop?

The answer lies in the "Tornado Effect."

Right now, pick up your rope. Hold one end and continuously twist it in the same direction as fast as you can. You will observe a phenomenon: as this twist-force runs forward, the circles grow larger and larger.

This is the Tornado Effect. Its root cause is that the twist initially rotates around the string's axis, but centrifugal tendency transforms it from simple spinning into a spiraling oscillation—which is the spiral pulse.

As a result, the spiral pulse at the very front, having oscillated the longest, is flung by centrifugal force into a large loop. The pulses behind it, formed later, still have smaller loops for the time being.

It is just like a tornado: the top is a giant funnel cloud, narrowing toward the bottom.

The large loop at the front has a longer circumference. Since the twist-force always runs at the speed of light, it takes longer to complete one circuit—it decelerates.

The slightly smaller loop behind it, though also slowed by the blocking of the preceding loop, is still slightly faster. After a few such progressions, the last, most recently twisted small tornado, with no time to decelerate, slams head-on into the already slowed loop ahead of it. This collision locks the trailing end to the leading end, and a closed loop is born.

You can think of it like a highway pileup.

The first car suddenly brakes. The second car, seeing this, also brakes, but the distance has already closed. The third car, seeing the second car brake, slams its brakes too, but is now even closer. By the time the fourth car reacts, it has no time to brake and rear-ends the third car.

The trailing end never needs to exceed the speed of light, because the leading end has already slowed itself down through the Tornado Effect.

Thus, when two high-energy light rays collide, they excite a violent string vibration between them. The vibration acts upon a perpendicular string, generating a spiral pulse. The spiral pulse twists, producing the Tornado Effect.

And just like that, a material loop is formed. This is an electron.

A single-loop structure is stable because its topology protects it, preventing it from being flattened directly by the string's tension on its two ends. But what happens if, after a single loop has formed, twist-force continues to pour in? The string below the loop will continue to twist, forming a long segment of "twist-pair."

However, once the topological loop at the top has formed, subsequent twist-force no longer generates new true loops. It merely piles up layer by layer into a twist-pair.

Every spiral intersection point of the twist-pair is a "twist-pair knot"—a place where two strings are wound around each other.

This is why all charged leptons have an electromagnetic force identical to that of the electron.

The masses of the muon and the tau are 207 times and 3,477 times that of the electron, respectively, yet their charge is always −1. This is because the muon and the tau are simply longer twist-pairs. The true loop at the top is always one and only one, so the electromagnetic force it radiates is naturally one and only one.

The electromagnetic force does not depend on quantity. The string-web trembles ceaselessly, and an electromagnetic effect exists only when the trembling is translated into a spiral wave by a topological loop.

As for gravity, it is the pull that a material loop exerts on both ends of the string it sits on.

A chain of twist-pairs stores more twist-force and elastic potential energy, so its pull on the string ends is naturally greater. But no matter how long the twist-pair, the charge is always determined solely by the topmost true loop.

## Matter Decay—The Disintegration of the Twist-Pair Loop

The tau and the muon disintegrate. Why? Because this twist-pair is inherently asymmetric. When it was generated, the twist-force did not pour in simultaneously from both ends of the string. It entered from only one end.

At the other end of the string, those tornadoes had long since run past and returned to being straight. The end where twist-force continuously poured in is the twisting string.

The formation of the twist-pair is essentially the twisting string coiling tightly around the straight string.

When a long twist-pair is finally fully formed, the two strings that compose it are asymmetric: one has almost no twist—this is the straight string; the other has stored a great deal of twist-force—this is the twisting string.

Assume the twist-force is transmitted from right to left. When the first topological loop forms, the loop itself is twisted. Its left side connects to the straight string, and its right side connects to the continuously twisting string.

The twisting string naturally wants to transfer force to the straight string. But the first topological loop has tied a twist-pair knot at the base, firmly blocking the incoming force.

This force has nowhere to go and can only react upon the twisting string itself—just like when you twist a fixed rubber band; once you let go, it spins back in the opposite direction.

This back-spinning is the driving force of decay—more precisely, the back-spinning of the twisting string drives the entire twist-pair to rotate.

Decay is not the action of a single force, but the interplay of two forces in opposite directions.

One is the anti-twist-force—the elastic rebound of the twist, driving the twisting string to back-spin and releasing a spiral pulse.

The other is the residual original twist-force—not yet released, still pushing upward, maintaining the existence of the twist-pair.

In one instant, the anti-twist-force is indeed stronger. It briefly bursts open the true loop at the top.

At the very moment the true loop is burst open, the twisting string and the straight string separate at the topmost point, forming an inverted U-shape—two strings lying parallel, no longer wound around each other. In this extremely brief window, a remnant of the original twist-force leaps from the twisting string into the straight string. However, when the two strings in the inverted U-shape are twisted in the same direction, they cannot form a twist-pair—you can verify this yourself.

The formation of a twist-pair requires a difference in twist-force between the two strings. Same-direction twisting only makes them spin individually and cannot lock them together. So this force does not lock the straight string and twisting string together. It simply enters the straight string and then travels downward along it.

The twist-pair knots further down the tau have just been opened by the anti-twist-force, leaving no obstruction. This force runs away completely unimpeded. After the force passes, the straight string returns to being straight.

This force is the muon pulse.

It escapes along the straight string. Its twist direction is the same as that of the muon—because it comes from the residual original twist-force, and its direction has never changed from beginning to end.

It is smaller than the tau anti-pulse—because it is a small remnant; most of the energy has already been carried away by the anti-twist-force.

On the naming of pulses and anti-pulses: A pulse has the same twist direction as the parent particle, corresponding to an antineutrino in standard physics. An anti-pulse has the opposite twist direction to the parent particle, corresponding to a neutrino in standard physics.

The pulse escapes along the straight string. The anti-pulse twists its way backward out of the twisting string.

After the pulse has escaped, the straight string returns to straight. The true loop re-closes—the residual original twist-force in the twisting string spins, dragging the entire upper portion to re-lock into a loop. Once the true loop is locked dead, twist-force can no longer enter the straight string.

The residual original twist-force is still within the twisting string. It twists from top to bottom, and over a shorter length, twists out a new, shorter twist-pair—this is the muon.

So you see, after the tau decays, a muon naturally forms at the upper end. This is all brought about by geometric motion.

And this is why when a tau decays into a muon, it releases a muon pulse (muon antineutrino), but a directly generated muon does not release a muon pulse.

Because during direct generation, the true loop is locked dead from the moment of creation. The inverted U-shape state never appears. The twist-force never has an opportunity to leap into the straight string and escape.

The reason decay involves a pulse is that the decay process itself requires the true loop to be briefly burst open—an inevitable consequence of the release of the anti-twist-force. At the instant the true loop is burst open, the inverted U-shape appears, and the twist-force seizes the chance to slip into the straight string and then escape unimpeded. This window never appears during direct generation.

When the tau completely disintegrates, a smaller twist-pair has already automatically formed above—the muon.

This new twist-pair is likewise asymmetric: the true loop at the top, although the first to be unbound, was also the first to re-form. After it re-forms, it blocks twist-force from entering the straight string from the twisting string.

Thus, the muon decays just the same. Its twisting string similarly back-spins in the opposite direction, releasing a muon anti-pulse—its direction opposite to that of the muon, and its formation mechanism exactly the same as the tau anti-pulse.

When the muon decays, its true loop is likewise briefly burst open. The inverted U-shape likewise appears. A remnant of the original twist-force likewise seizes the chance to slip into the straight string and then escape via the straight string. This is the electron pulse (electron antineutrino), with the same direction as the electron.

When the muon completely disintegrates, what the final residual twist-force forms is a single loop—the electron. The electron has no twist-pair, and no twist-density differential. Therefore, it never decays.

Whether it is the tau or the muon, the end result of decay can only be an electron.

Why is the tau's twist-pair so much longer than the muon's, yet its decay speed is so much faster? The tau disintegrates in just three trillionths of a second, while the muon, though much shorter, takes two millionths of a second.

Because the decay speed depends on the twist-density differential between the twisting string and the straight string (which is essentially the elastic potential energy).

This is like drawing a bow: a fully drawn bow may be pulled further, but it stores greater elastic potential energy, and the speed of its release is much faster than a bow pulled only slightly.

As for why the muon's mass is exactly 207 times the electron's, and the tau's is 3,477 times—frankly, I do not know. It is probably determined by the string's elastic constant k.
