Einstein said that the stronger the gravity, the slower the time. What is the explanation for this?

**Gravity and Time**

We already know that gravity is the elastic pull a material loop exerts on the two ends of the string it sits on. Massive celestial bodies—like the Earth or the Sun—are composed of countless material loops. These loops collectively tighten the surrounding string-web. The stronger the gravity, the denser the string-web is pulled, and the shorter the spacing between the perpendicular intersection points of countless strings.

In a strong gravitational field, the string-web density increases, and the density of perpendicular intersection points increases. A beam of light traveling from point A to point B might need to pass only a hundred perpendicular intersection points in a flat string-web. But in a strong gravitational field, covering the same physical distance might require passing a thousand perpendicular intersection points.

Every time light passes a perpendicular intersection point, it undergoes a cycle of energy borrowing and returning. The perpendicular string is pushed up by the wave crest, storing elastic and torsional potential energy, and then returns the energy during the wave's downhill phase.

Although the net energy loss is zero for each cycle, every cycle takes time. The more perpendicular intersection points passed, the greater the total number of cycles, and the longer the actual "effective path" the light travels.

Following this derivation, the frequency of light actually drops as the string-web density increases. As we deduced in Chapter 4, light is pressed down slightly each time it passes a perpendicular intersection point, causing its frequency to drop. A denser string-web means passing more perpendicular intersection points, and a faster drop in frequency.

This is, in fact, the phenomenon of gravitational redshift. (Light's frequency drops under strong gravity.)

Returning to light itself: the speed of light has not changed. The speed at which light runs on the string is always determined by the string's elastic constant k. This is invariant.

However, because it interacts among more nodes, its total journey—the "work it does"—is lengthened. Therefore, the total time is also lengthened.

If you stand beside a massive celestial body, very close to the light, you will feel that the speed of light has not changed at all—because the clock beside you is also running just as slow. The speed of light you measure is the speed of light.

But if you stand far away and watch a beam of light pass near a massive celestial body, you will perceive that beam of light as having slowed down. Not because the speed of light has changed, but because the light has passed through a region dense with nodes, and its actual path is longer than it appears from afar.

The light has traveled an extra stretch of road you cannot see, so you perceive it as slower. This is the root of gravitational time dilation, and also the root of gravitational lensing.

Gravity causes the perceived speed of light to decrease—in reality, by making the light travel a longer distance. And why does gravity also cause the speed of the electromagnetic force to drop?

**The Two Stages of the Electromagnetic Force**

More precisely, the speed of the electromagnetic force is composed of two parts: first, the rate at which it is emitted from the loop; second, the rate at which it propagates through the string-web after emission.

Special Relativity governs the "emission." When a loop moves at high speed, the loop's own velocity crowds out the share of its internal vibration. The faster the loop runs, the less room is left for internal vibration, and the frequency at which the loop's twisting geometry radiates spiral waves drops. This is the electromagnetic force being suppressed at its source.

General Relativity governs the "propagation."

After being emitted from the loop, the electromagnetic force runs through the string-web in the form of a spiral wave. Just like light, it undergoes energy borrowing and returning at perpendicular intersection points.

In a strong gravitational field, the string-web is pulled denser, creating more perpendicular intersection points. Every time the spiral wave passes a perpendicular intersection point, it undergoes a cycle of energy borrowing and returning, and every cycle takes time.

Over the same distance, more nodes are passed, so the total time is longer. This is the electromagnetic force being delayed on its journey.

Thus, in a strong gravitational field, the actual speed of the electromagnetic force drops.

Similarly, when a material loop moves along a string, it too must undergo energy borrowing and returning, just like light. Therefore, the movement speed of the material loop itself also drops.

Of course, more importantly, the spatial micro-tremor also requires energy borrowing and returning. So, as spatial density increases, the propagation efficiency of the spatial micro-tremor drops. This leads to a drop in the frequency of the very source of the electromagnetic force.

One is a slowing of the vibration. The other is a slowing of the propagation. Special Relativity and General Relativity thus act upon these two separate stages of the electromagnetic force, jointly forming our complete explanation for the "slowing of time."

It is important to note that the "stronger gravity, slower time" effect only applies to material loops, the electromagnetic force, light, spiral pulses, spatial micro-tremors, string waves, and gravitational waves. It does not apply to gravity itself.

Where gravity is stronger, an apple falls faster.

This is because the propagation of gravity does not require energy borrowing and returning through perpendicular intersection points. A denser string-web does not reduce the efficiency of gravity. Quite the opposite: the stronger the gravity, the faster an object is pulled.

Although a material loop decelerates with greater spatial density due to more cycles of energy borrowing and returning, the increase in gravity outpaces the increase in spatial density. So, overall, gravitational efficiency increases with mass.
