

We now know what matter is. Let us explain gravity.

First, a fundamental distinction must be made: only matter has gravity. Energy does not.

What is the difference? Energy is a free bend running along a string. Matter, on the other hand, is a bend locked dead inside a topological soliton—trapped in place.

This topological soliton is an unbreakable loop upon the string.

Because the string has elasticity, it naturally wants to return to a straight line. It therefore exerts a pulling force on both ends of the loop, trying to flatten out this twisted "knot."

But the geometric structure (topology) of the knot protects it, making it impossible to straighten.

As a result, this elastic pull from the string transforms into a reaction force, permanently applied to the base of the loop, and transmitted along the string to both ends.

This pulling force, originating from the base of the loop and exerted upon the string, is gravity.

In other words, gravity is the elastic pull that a topological loop exerts on the two ends of the string it sits upon.

But this pull does not propagate infinitely. Because the string itself has elasticity, as the pull travels along it—like tugging on a stretchy rubber band—the force is gradually absorbed and dissipated by the string's own deformation. This causes gravity to decay with distance. The farther from the material loop, the more the string's tension is consumed.

From this, we reach an important conclusion: the longer the string locked within a material loop (i.e., the greater its mass), the stronger its pull on the two ends of the string, and therefore the stronger the gravity it manifests.

But here lies a critical question: if gravity is just a one-directional pull along the string, why does gravity in our reality—such as the Earth's gravity—act in all directions, like a sphere?

This is because the material loop is not static. At every moment, it is engaged in high-speed random jumps driven by the string-web's background tremor, billions of times per second.

It not only moves along its own string, but continuously jumps onto perpendicular strings as well.

This means that within a single second, the loop generates trillions of instantaneous pulls on the ends of countless strings in different directions.

On a macroscopic scale, these rapidly changing pulls from all directions are averaged into a perfect, spherical gravitational field acting in every direction. The spherical action of gravity is therefore a statistical averaging effect produced by the loop's high-speed jumping across the string-web.

Let us use this mechanism to explain the mutual attraction of gravity.

Imagine two material loops. One is very small—let us call it the "Small Loop," and assign its gravity a value of "1." The other is very large—the "Large Loop," with gravity "10."

Suppose they settle on the same string, with the Small Loop on the left and the Large Loop on the right.

The Small Loop exerts a pull of 1 on the left end of the string, and also a pull of 1 on the right end. The Large Loop exerts a pull of 10 on the left end, and 10 on the right end.

Now, on the segment of string between the Small Loop and the Large Loop, the Small Loop pulls to the right and the Large Loop pulls to the left. These two forces superimpose, creating a total tension of 1 + 10 = 11. They are like two people grabbing opposite ends of the same rope, pulling each other closer. This is the manifestation of gravitational mutual attraction.

But interestingly, the speed at which the two loops approach each other is different.

Let us examine this: the Small Loop feels a pull of 1 on its left side and a pull of 11 on its right side (because the string segment between it and the Large Loop bears the total tension). So the net force on the Small Loop is 10 to the right.

For the Large Loop, it feels a pull of 11 on its left side and a pull of 10 on its right side. Its net force is 1 to the left.

As you can see, the Small Loop is pulled by a net force of "10," while the Large Loop is pulled by a net force of only "1."

This means that as they approach each other, the speed at which the Small Loop accelerates toward the Large Loop will be ten times the speed at which the Large Loop accelerates toward the Small Loop.

The smaller the mass (the shorter the locked string) of an object, the more pronounced its acceleration toward a massive object under gravity.

This perfectly explains why an apple rapidly hurtles toward the Earth, while the Earth barely moves.

**Buoyancy? Why does water lift a boat?**

In String-Space, buoyancy does not exist.

It is not that water lifts the boat. It is that water sinks deeper than the boat.

Objects under gravity all fall toward the center of the Earth. The denser an object, the more gravitational pull it receives per unit volume, giving it a greater "priority right to fall."

So, a piece of iron placed on water sinks because iron, being denser, possesses the priority right to fall.

And when you place a piece of foam on water, it is not that the water lifts the foam. It is that water, being denser, possesses the priority right to fall.

Therefore, buoyancy does not exist. It is simply a matter of which has greater density ends up below.

Just like a piece of iron will, over time, slowly sink deep into the soil, deep toward the Earth's core, because its density is greater and it experiences a greater gravitational force per unit volume.

But why doesn't the iron simply fall right through the soil when placed on it?

Because the soil possesses electromagnetic force. This electromagnetic force makes the soil compact, preventing the iron from penetrating straight through it.
